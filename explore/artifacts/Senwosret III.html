About the artifact

This seated granite colossus captures Thutmose III at the height of his power — a king who built Egypt's largest empire and whose military genius earned him the title "Napoleon of Ancient Egypt" from modern historians.

The king sits on a royal throne with the composure of a man who has never lost a battle. His hands rest palms-down on his knees — a pose of controlled authority, neither aggressive nor passive. He wears the nemes headcloth and the shendyt royal kilt. Beneath his feet, carved into the base, are the Nine Bows — the traditional symbol of Egypt's enemies, permanently crushed under the weight of the throne.

Thutmose III came to power as a child, forced to share the throne with his stepmother Hatshepsut for 22 years while she held the full powers of pharaoh. After her death, he ruled alone — and made up for lost time. He led at least 17 military campaigns into Syria, Canaan, and Nubia. His decisive victory at the Battle of Megiddo, one of the earliest battles recorded in detailed historical accounts, became a masterclass in military strategy still studied today.

Discovered at Karnak in 1859, this statue stood at the very temple complex where Thutmose III inscribed his military annals on the walls — a monument to a king who carved his record into both stone and history.
