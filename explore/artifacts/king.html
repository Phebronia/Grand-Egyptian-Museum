About the artifact

Carved from red granite and standing five metres tall, this colossus depicts a Ptolemaic king in the traditional Egyptian style — a Greek-born ruler portrayed with the full iconography of a pharaoh, wearing the double crown of Upper and Lower Egypt and the royal shendyt kilt.

The king stands with his left leg advanced, arms held firmly at his sides, his right fist gripping a small cylindrical object — a convention inherited from Old Kingdom royal statuary more than two millennia earlier. Though the inscriptions have not survived, the scale, style, and provenance of the statue point most strongly to Ptolemy II Philadelphus, who ruled Egypt between 285 and 246 BC.

The colossus originally stood outside a temple of Amun-Gereb at Thonis-Heracleion, the legendary port city that was Egypt's main gateway to the Mediterranean for centuries. Then, around the 8th century AD, earthquakes and rising sea levels sent the entire city sinking beneath the waters of Abu Qir Bay off the coast of Alexandria — and the colossus went with it.

In the year 2000, a joint mission led by the European Institute for Underwater Archaeology rediscovered the sunken city. Divers found the colossus lying on the seabed more than 6 kilometres offshore, together with its companion statue of a Ptolemaic queen — likely Arsinoe II, portrayed as the goddess Isis. The two statues toured Europe and the United States as part of the "Sunken Cities" exhibition before taking their permanent place in the atrium of the Grand Egyptian Museum.

A Greek king in pharaoh's clothing — and a city the sea tried to keep.
