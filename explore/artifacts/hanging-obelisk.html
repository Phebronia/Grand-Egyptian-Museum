About the artifact

Standing 16 meters tall and weighing 87 tons, the Hanging Obelisk is the first artefact to greet visitors at the Grand Egyptian Museum — and the first obelisk in the world to be displayed suspended in mid-air.

Carved from a single block of red granite quarried at Aswan, the obelisk was originally raised by Ramses II at Tanis in the eastern Nile Delta, where the pharaoh erected more than ten such monuments. It is the tallest obelisk ever to have stood at that ancient city, and its four sides bear inscriptions proclaiming Ramses's Horus name, throne name, and birth name — along with epithets like "Beloved of Amun" and "Strong Bull."

Discovered broken in fragments during 20th-century excavations, the obelisk was transported to Giza for painstaking restoration. During that work, archaeologists noticed something extraordinary on the underside of its base: a rare cartouche of Ramses II, carved in a place that would normally be hidden forever once the obelisk was raised. The king had likely inscribed it there to safeguard his name from future rulers who might try to usurp his monuments by re-carving the visible faces.

That hidden cartouche inspired the museum's architects to do what had never been done before — elevate the obelisk on a four-column steel frame so that visitors could walk beneath it, look up through a glass floor, and read an inscription invisible for over three and a half thousand years. Advanced base isolators shield the 87-ton monument from the vibrations of nearby traffic and the Cairo Metro, making preservation and spectacle coexist.

An inscription hidden for 3,500 years, now visible above the heads of those who walk beneath it.
