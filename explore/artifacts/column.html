About the artifact

Carved from red granite on a limestone base, this 5.6-metre column commemorates the military victories of King Merenptah — the thirteenth son and eventual successor of Ramses II — and the moment the name "Israel" first appears in world history.

By the time he took the throne in 1213 BC, Merenptah was already elderly, having outlived most of his brothers during his father's 66-year reign. His own decade of rule was short but turbulent: Egypt's western frontier was under attack from Libyan tribes allied with the mysterious Sea Peoples, and unrest was spreading across the Levant. This column records his victories over them.

Four sides of the shaft are covered in scenes of royal power. Merenptah is shown receiving a mace from the warrior goddess Anath, and the sword of victory from Amun, Re-Horakhty, Seth, Montu, and the lioness goddess Sekhmet. Bearded captives are bound with ropes just above the base, and the inscription records the spoils of battle — gold, silver, horses, goats, bows and arrows — taken from those who had "transgressed the borders of Egypt."

The column originally stood at a temple Merenptah built at Heliopolis — the ancient City of the Sun, today buried beneath the Cairo suburb of Matariya. Discovered in 1970, it was taken to Cairo's Salaheddin Citadel in 2008 for a decade of conservation, as rising groundwater was threatening the surrounding area. In 2018 it was transported to its permanent home in the atrium of the Grand Egyptian Museum, where it stands today among the colossi of Ramses II and the Ptolemaic kings.

Take your mace so that you may kill the rebels.

— The goddess Anath, addressing Merenptah
