About the artifact

The Golden Throne of Tutankhamun is one of the most exquisite pieces of furniture ever recovered from ancient Egypt — a ceremonial chair carved from wood and entirely covered in sheet gold and silver.

Inlaid with coloured glass, faience, and semi-precious stones, the throne is celebrated above all for the tender scene on its backrest: Queen Ankhesenamun anoints her young husband with perfumed oil, both bathed in the rays of the sun-disk Aten. It is a rare, intimate glimpse into royal life — an image of affection rather than authority.

The throne's arms are formed as winged uraei — protective cobra goddesses crowned with sun-disks — and its legs take the shape of lion's paws, symbolizing royal power and protection. Hieroglyphic inscriptions on the throne bear both of Tutankhamun's names, including his earlier name Tutankhaten, reflecting the religious transition of his reign.

Discovered in the Antechamber of tomb KV62 in 1922, Howard Carter described it as "the most beautiful thing that has yet been found in Egypt." Today, the throne stands as a masterpiece of New Kingdom craftsmanship and a window into the personal world of the boy king.
