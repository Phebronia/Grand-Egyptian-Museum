Inherited Conditions

Direct consequences of generational royal inbreeding

Clubfoot

Talipes Equinovarus

A congenital foot deformity twisting the foot inward. Forced the king to rely on walking sticks — over 130 were found in his tomb.

Köhler Disease II

Avascular Necrosis

Bone death caused by disrupted blood supply to the metatarsals. Found in the second and third metatarsal bones of the left foot.

Cleft Palate

Palatoschisis

A split in the roof of the mouth present from birth. A known marker of congenital abnormality common in offspring of closely related parents.

Weakened Immunity

Immune Deficiency

Inbreeding reduces genetic diversity, compromising the immune response. Tutankhamun carried at least four strains of malaria — suggesting a severely weakened defence system.

☥ ANCIENT EGYPT'S PLAGUES ☥

Diseases of the Pharaohs' Era

The ancient world was rife with infectious and parasitic illness — many confirmed in royal mummies

Malaria

Plasmodium falciparum

The deadliest form of malaria, spread by Anopheles mosquitoes thriving along the Nile. Four strains of P. falciparum DNA were found in Tutankhamun's remains — the oldest confirmed malaria infection in recorded history.

Confirmed in Tutankhamun

Schistosomiasis

Schistosoma haematobium

A parasitic disease caused by flatworms living in Nile water. Eggs found in several Egyptian mummies. The parasite caused chronic internal bleeding, organ damage, and severe fatigue — a silent killer that weakened the population for millennia.

Widespread in the era

Tuberculosis

Mycobacterium tuberculosis

Evidence of TB has been found in Egyptian mummies dating back to 3000 BC. The disease attacked the lungs and spine, leaving distinctive lesions on vertebrae. While not confirmed in Tutankhamun himself, TB was endemic throughout the Nile Valley during the New Kingdom.

Widespread in the era

Parasitic Infections

Multiple species

Studies of ancient Egyptian remains have documented roundworm, tapeworm, and Guinea worm infections. Contaminated water and food made parasitic disease ubiquitous across all social classes — royalty included. These chronic infections depleted nutrition and suppressed immunity over time.

Widespread in the era

Atherosclerosis

Arterial calcification

CT scans of 52 Egyptian mummies from multiple eras revealed calcified arteries consistent with heart disease — proving that atherosclerosis is not a modern condition. A high-salt, high-fat diet combined with genetic predisposition made it common among the elite class who could afford meat-rich diets.

Widespread in the era

Severe Dental Disease

Dental attrition & abscess

Sand-contaminated bread ground on stone mills wore Egyptian teeth to the pulp. Almost every adult mummy shows severe dental attrition. Tooth abscesses were common causes of fatal infection. Ramses II suffered from a severe abscess — potentially life-threatening at his advanced age.

Widespread in the era

☥ A CENTURY OF DISCOVERY ☥

The Research Timeline

From Howard Carter's torch to modern DNA sequencing — a hundred years of uncovering the truth

1922

Discovery of KV62

Howard Carter and Lord Carnarvon unseal the nearly intact tomb of Tutankhamun in the Valley of the Kings. Over 5,000 objects are catalogued over the next decade — including 130+ walking canes that would later hint at the king's mobility problems.

1925

Derry's First Autopsy

Anatomist Douglas Derry performs the first full examination of the royal mummy. He estimates the king was 18–22 years old at death and notes bone immaturity. Skeletal abnormalities go largely unrecognised at this stage.

1968

X-Ray Study

A University of Liverpool team X-rays the mummy and finds loose bone fragments inside the skull, sparking decades of debate about a possible blow to the head. Later CT analysis would reveal these fragments likely shifted post-mummification during transport.

2005

CT Scan Project

The Egyptian Supreme Council of Antiquities conducts a full CT scan of the mummy — 1,700 cross-sectional images. The team identifies a left leg fracture shortly before death, avascular necrosis in multiple bones, and the clubfoot deformity. The murder hypothesis is largely dismissed.

2010

DNA Study — JAMA

Zahi Hawass leads a landmark study published in the Journal of the American Medical Association. DNA from 11 royal mummies establishes a five-generation family tree. The study confirms: Tutankhamun's parents were siblings, he carried four malaria strains, and he had multiple bone diseases. The conclusion: death from a combination of malaria and complications from a leg fracture.

2014

Virtual Autopsy

A BBC documentary team uses 2,000 CT scans to build a virtual 3D model of Tutankhamun's body — the most detailed reconstruction to date. The model confirms a severely deformed left foot, multiple inherited conditions, and a complex of injuries. A new theory emerges: the king may have been struck by a chariot wheel, fracturing his chest and leg.

☥ SCHOLARLY SOURCES ☥

References

All findings on this page are drawn from peer-reviewed research and primary archaeological sources

Hawass, Z., Gad, Y. Z., Ismail, S., et al. (2010). Ancestry and Pathology in King Tutankhamun's Family. JAMA, 303(7), 638–647. doi:10.1001/jama.2010.121

The landmark study that identified malaria, bone disease, and sibling parentage using DNA from 11 royal mummies. The primary source for most modern findings about Tutankhamun's health.

Selim, A., & Elhadidy, M. (2006). The CT Study of Tutankhamun. In Hawass, Z. (Ed.), Tutankhamun and the Golden Age of the Pharaohs. National Geographic Society. (CT imaging data collected 2005.)

1,700 CT cross-sections produced from the 2005 Egyptian Mummy Project. Identified the clubfoot, bone necrosis, and the left leg fracture that likely occurred close to death.

Selim, A., et al. (2014). Whole body CT of Tutankhamun: a virtual autopsy. Prepared for the BBC documentary Tutankhamun: The Truth Uncovered. 2,000 CT scans used to generate a 3D anatomical model.

The most detailed post-mortem reconstruction yet. Confirmed a scoliosis-like spinal curvature, the deformed left foot, and a complex chest fracture. Introduced the chariot accident hypothesis.

Harrison, R. G., Connolly, R. C., & Abdalla, A. (1969). Kinship of Smenkhkare and Tutankhamun demonstrated serologically. Nature, 224, 325–326. And: Harrison, R. G. (1966). An anatomical examination of the pharaonic remains purported to be Akhenaten. Journal of Egyptian Archaeology, 52, 95–119.

The first systematic radiological study of the mummy. Identified loose bone fragments in the skull, fuelling the murder debate. Blood type O was determined — later disputed as unreliable from degraded samples.

Derry, D. E. (1927). Report on the Human Remains. In Carter, H. & Mace, A. C. (Eds.), The Tomb of Tut·ankh·Amen, Vol. II. Cassell & Company, London. pp. 143–161.

The first formal anatomical report on the mummy. Estimated age at death as 18–22 years. Limited by the tools of the time but remained the primary reference for decades.

Allam, A. H., Thompson, R. C., Wann, L. S., et al. (2011). Atherosclerosis in Ancient Egyptian Mummies: The Horus Study. JACC Cardiovascular Imaging, 4(4), 315–327. doi:10.1016/j.jcmg.2011.02.002

CT scanning of 52 ancient Egyptian mummies across multiple dynasties — evidence of atherosclerosis in 45% of subjects, proving cardiovascular disease predates modern lifestyle factors.
