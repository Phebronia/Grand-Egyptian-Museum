Level 1

Royal Image

The ascent begins with the face of kingship itself. Colossal seated and standing statues of pharaohs — from Senwosret III of the Middle Kingdom to Hatshepsut, Thutmose III, Amenhotep III, and even the Roman emperor Caracalla portrayed as pharaoh — show how each ruler chose to present himself to gods, subjects, and eternity.

Level 2

Divine Houses

Here the temples themselves come into focus. Obelisk tips, temple lintels, and architectural fragments — including a gilded tip from an obelisk of Hatshepsut — recall the great sanctuaries of Karnak, Luxor, and beyond, where the pharaoh built permanence into stone for the gods.

Level 3

Kings & Gods

Midway up the staircase, the pharaoh meets the divine. Statues depict kings standing, kneeling, or embracing the gods they served — Sekhmet the lioness goddess of war, Ptah the creator, and Re-Horakhty the morning sun — illustrating the sacred bond between the ruler and the powers of the cosmos.

Level 4

Journey to Eternity

The final ascent is funerary. Stone sarcophagi from across three millennia and a lone pyramidion from an unknown 13th Dynasty king at Saqqara prepare the visitor for the last step — a panoramic window opening onto the Giza Pyramids, the eternal houses of the kings.

Rising six stories through 6,000 square metres, the Grand Staircase is both architecture and narrative — a climb from the earliest kings to the splendour of the New Kingdom, ending with the pyramids themselves framed in glass.
