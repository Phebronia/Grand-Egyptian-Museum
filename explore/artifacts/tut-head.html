About the artifact

Sculpted from wood and painted with remarkable delicacy, this small head shows the young Tutankhamun rising from the open petals of a blue lotus — a symbol of rebirth and the first moment of creation in Egyptian belief.

In the ancient Egyptian creation myth, the sun-god first emerged as a child from a lotus flower that rose out of the primeval waters of Nun. By depicting the king in this form, the sculpture identifies Tutankhamun with the sun at the instant of its daily rebirth — an act of divine renewal intended to accompany the king into the afterlife.

The features are rendered with striking naturalism: the elongated skull, full cheeks, and soft expression reflect the late Amarna artistic style inherited from the reign of his father, Akhenaten. The surface was carved from a single piece of wood, then coated with plaster and painted with fine detail.

Found in the corridor of tomb KV62 in 1922, the piece was among the first objects Howard Carter encountered upon entering the tomb. Today it is regarded as one of the most poetic and visually arresting works from the entire Tutankhamun collection — an intimate portrait that captures both the fragility and the divinity of the boy king.

The king rises like the sun from the eternal lotus.

— From the Egyptian funerary texts
