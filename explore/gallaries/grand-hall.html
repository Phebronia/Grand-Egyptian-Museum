c. 1250 BC

Carved in Aswan

A single block of red granite was quarried at Aswan and carved into an 11-metre statue of Ramses II, the third pharaoh of the Nineteenth Dynasty. It was transported down the Nile and raised at the Temple of Ptah in the ancient capital of Memphis, where it would stand for more than three thousand years.

1820

Rediscovered in Six Pieces

Italian explorer Giovanni Battista Caviglia — the same archaeologist who excavated the Great Sphinx — found the statue lying on its side at Mit Rahina, broken into six fragments. Offered to an Italian duke and then to the British Museum, both declined as too costly to move. The colossus remained in the sand for another 135 years.

1955

Rising Over Ramses Square

Under Egyptian Prime Minister Gamal Abdel Nasser, the fragments were restored and raised outside Cairo's main railway station. For half a century the colossus stood as a silent sentinel in the heart of the capital — surrounded by traffic and slowly corroded by the city's pollution.

2018

Home at the GEM

After a ten-hour overnight journey through Cairo in 2006, and a decade of restoration at Giza, the colossus was placed in its permanent home — the atrium of the Grand Egyptian Museum. The building itself was designed around the statue, since it could not have fit through any completed door. Today it stands beneath a glass roof, illuminated at equinox by a beam of sunlight that echoes the ancient alignment at Abu Simbel.

Spanning 10,000 square metres beneath a translucent alabaster façade, the Grand Hall is the threshold between the Nile Valley and the Giza Plateau — a space where the visitor stands, for a moment, between the world of the kings and the world of today.
