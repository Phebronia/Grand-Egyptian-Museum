About the artifact

Hatshepsut was one of ancient Egypt's most successful pharaohs — a woman who declared herself king, wore a false beard, and ruled the most powerful empire on earth for more than twenty years.

Born around 1507 BC, Hatshepsut was the daughter of Thutmose I and queen consort to Thutmose II. When her husband died, she became regent for her young stepson Thutmose III — but within a few years she had claimed the full titles and regalia of pharaoh, presenting herself in sculpture and relief wearing the double crown, royal kilt, and braided false beard that marked the divine kingship.

Her reign was defined not by war but by trade and building. She sent a celebrated expedition to the land of Punt, returning with myrrh trees, ivory, ebony, and exotic animals. She erected two towering obelisks at Karnak — the tallest in the world at the time — and built her magnificent mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahari, regarded as one of the greatest architectural achievements of ancient Egypt.

After her death, Thutmose III ordered many of her images erased from monuments and her statues smashed — an attempt to erase her from history. She was forgotten for centuries. Only in the 19th century did scholars decoding hieroglyphs reveal that she had been female, restoring Hatshepsut to her rightful place as one of Egypt's most extraordinary rulers.

I am the king's daughter. I am the king's wife. I am the king.

— Inscriptions of Hatshepsut
